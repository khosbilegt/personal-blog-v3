## Thoughts

Cradle by [Will Wight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Will_Wight) is one of the best pieces of English Xianxia/Cultivation fiction I've read and would recommend to anyone interested in fantasy. It melds Eastern fantasy elements and merges them with more typical Western elements, which makes it easy to process for most Western audiences and for the better or worse, it insulates new readers from the more extreme cases of cultural difference.

It effectively becomes the best gateway to reading Cultivation novels and the fact that each individual novel isn't massive on their own, but structured as a part of a large series with digestible novels for each arc makes it surprisingly addictive. For a genre that is more widely known for its neverending nature and webnovel format that keeps on going in an episodic manner, it's a welcome breath of fresh air.

## Characters:

- Lindon (Male Protagonist): Lindon starts his life as a clever, but "untalented" fellow. He carries over the typical surface-level respectful nature that Cultivation novel protagonists are known for having, and if not for the rest of the main cast, would make Cradle a far more Eastern-oriented piece of fiction. While respectful, it doesn't stop him from being ruthless, or an absolute mad lad, but that's par for the course and true to the genre.

- Yerin (Female Protagonist): Yerin, on the other hand, is what a typical Young Adult protagonist would be like. Not the kind that's badass just because, but the kind of badass that has the background for it and keeps on proving they are. Considering that she's the other main protagonist of the series, it makes for a nice blend of Eastern and Western protagonists keeping each others' influences in check so that it becomes a mix of both.

- Eithan (Mentor Figure): Eithan is the mentor figure, and their arc plays out the way it would usually, but he plays a more central role to the plot compared to most mentor figures who appear for brief periods of time, then conveniently disappear whether by demise or complications. It's another subversion to the common trope that I welcome.

- Orthos (Mascot): Mythical creatures as pets is a staple of cultivation novels, and unlike most novels where they're forgotten or left behind for large periods of time, Orthos also plays a central role in the story and I'm all in for a grumpy, grandpa-style turtle being around as the mascot.

As for the side cast, they are also quite unique, and while there are undertones of Cultivation elements, they are also changed and tweaked so as to become original and different enough from typical Cultivation novels; and become recurring characters.

## Worldbuilding

In most Cultivation novels, you start from an inferior realm and then ascend to the upper realm upon growing stronger. This hierarchy is apparent from the very first few chapters of the story, wherein an Immortal descends from a higher plane of existence and proves their existence, starting Lindon on his path. The events in the upper realm are shown as a side-plot that augments the original story in ways that aren't usually shown, revealing elements in Lindon's realm that are related to a certain Immortal from the upper realm. (Note that the terminology used are Xianxia terms rather than Cradle-specific terms for brevity)

The best part is that this is in the same world as the author's other works, such as [Traveler's Gate](https://www.goodreads.com/series/108461-traveler-s-gate) and [Elder Empire](https://www.goodreads.com/series/169166-the-elder-empire-sea). For fans of Cultivation, it means that they share the same upper realm but are all set in lower realms within the same multiverse. That's also something that is rarely seen in Western books, more the territory of comic books.

**Cultivation**

Cultivation in Cradle has standard elements but incorporates different elements, such as bonding with Sacred Beasts being a stage; and the most unique of which is the existence of Remnants, which becomes a part of the cultivation pathway. Remnants are left behind when a Cultivator dies. They are then absorbed by a Cultivator, and leaves a permanent imprint on them, such as becoming extra limbs, growing wings, or permanently attached armor. On the higher scale, it then goes back to the standard evolution of affecting laws of physics and reality bending, which are presented as impressive as they should be.

**Power System**

This is where the Western elements bleed in. The powers are treated as they would in Japanese anime/manga or Western comics rather than in Cultivation novels. While in most Cultivation novels attacks are flashy but without much substance other than concussive force, with slight differences when it comes to niche powers like poison, in Cradle, abilities & their elements are fully taken advantage of in every way possible. This is reminiscent of early Naruto where every technique has a certain level of interactivity with opponents, their attacks and the world; but the main difference is that this interactivity is ramped up as the series goes on instead of devolving to all flash, no substance.

**Dreadgods**

I don't know whether this is because I am drawing parallels with [Worm](https://parahumans.wordpress.com/) by Wildbow, but the existence of Dreadgods is very similar to the Endbringers from Worm. They're living catastrophes and are treated as such, essentially Godzillas that break havoc across the world. It's a welcome addition to a genre that usually depicts these creatures hiding away in a random forest to jumpscare the protagonist, then fighting each other and dying in the process, only to become resources for the protagonist. Really glad that they are given due attention and their fates play out differently than the norm.

## Plotline

One thing I absolutely dig about Cradle is the brevity of it. Things happen, and within what doesn't seem like a long time, the main cast reach the top in a believable fashion. The world changes around them, reacts to them and adapts to them. Actions have consequences, but rarely do illogical things happen. People don't bump into the main cast and just decide to kill them for the smallest offence --they just happen to be in a place that killing is a viable solution to a certain problem.

## Conclusion

I essentially feel that this is the bridge between Young Adult, Urban Fantasy into full-on Cultivation novels and it feels quite polished.
