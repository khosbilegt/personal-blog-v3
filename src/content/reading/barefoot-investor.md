## Reflection

Shortly after I landed in Australia, my uncle recommended the book to me to teach me about the investment landscape of Australia and generally how I could live a plentiful life by investing properly. As someone who had been living rent-free back at home and had built up a habit of saving much of my income, the idea of 'buckets' was something I'd naturally come to the conclusion of. My mindset was that I'd just have 3 accounts: checking, rainy day fund and long-term savings. I usually kept the amount I knew I'd have to spend to survive, then added roughly 50% to my savings and the rest to my rainy day fund which helped me build up a decent savings account.

Oddly enough, the notion of the "rainy day fund" actually came from watching Breaking Bad when Jesse Pinkman explained the concept. Before that, I just had a checking and savings account. At that point, I hadn't become comfortable enough to spend money on the stock market and still am not (though now due to lack of funds after moving out).

Understanding the debt system was quite easy, but perhaps that is because I've never been in debt before and hadn't dealt with the stress. Maybe I will at some point, probably for a mortgage or a car payment, but it went into the "I'll do it later" section of the to-do list instead of "I should do it now" part.

I think the best takeaway was automating transfers. As someone that believes reducing friction from everyday life to save energy for the important bits, it was a no-brainer to start implementing it immediately. As a result, I strapped my bank app to save a part of my salary for my tuition fee, another account for rent and then my checking account for splurging. At the moment, I don't have money to splurge so that was the logical conclusion.

The rest were largely irrelevant to a single college student (so no date nights) and I haven't built up enough of a savings account, and likely will not until I graduate, and will likely have me revisit it once I am in a better financial predicament. Then again, maybe I should treat it as paying off my student loans in real time and be proud of it?
