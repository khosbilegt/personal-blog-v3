## Reflection

This book was always lying on my dad's side of the bed counter when I was in high school. I saw it whenever I was cleaning the house. At some point, it moved from his bedside and into his bookshelf. That's when I snatched it to give it a read. While in retrospect, it was obviously a book about investments, I thought it was some nonsense about comparing dads by someone that had a messy family situation.

While I don't remember much about my initial thoughts, also in retrospect, I find that it's taught me the importance of investing in assets rather than showing off; and just trying to get a house as fast as possible. It explained in excruciating detail why the housing market is the way it is, and the fact that it'll keep on getting more expensive with each passing day, and now that I'm writing this reflection, sitting in my rental, I feel that he was right.

I dislike the notion of a human necessity being turned into a revenue-generating stream, but the game is there, everyone else is playing it and I can't flip the board like a child and declare that I'd won. I have to learn the game, play it and win it.

Thinking back, maybe my dad has been pecking away at the book all along while raising me, because I vividly remember buying my PlayStation 2 by using the money I gathered from New Years (our culture has a tradition of placing money on the Christmas tree when visiting) and then going 50-50 with my dad to purchase my Xbox 360 years later. Now that I think about it, maybe it was his attempt at teaching me about equity and raising investments.

He did end up raising me with more of an awareness about money than my peers from way back in elementary school, even the so-proclaimed poor ones who always had a relatively new model of a smartphone and the rich ones who splurged on two meals after class. That spurred me to build my first business back in middle school:

- Basically, we had a 20 minute lunch break. It wasn't really a lunch break. The idea was that they'd bring food into the classroom consisting of tea and some pastry, and we'd eat in the classroom. However, due to funding issues, that didn't happen for anyone other than elementary kids.
- Seeing as we were in middle school, we had to race to the other side of the school, say 300 meters, queue up at the school cafeteria, grab some food and only if we were lucky and found that there was no line, we'd eat there. Obviously if there was a line, the cafeteria that was roughly a 5x10 meter area would be full. Then we would have to gobble up whatever we had and run back, or risk being yelled at by the teacher.
- You might have figured out the idea by now: I decided to charge a small fee for others, run to the cafeteria first thing, then come running back with their foods. Then I usually took a couple stops on the way to have my own lunch.

Technically, since we started school at around 7AM and ended around 2PM, if we had a breakfast in the morning, we didn't actually have to eat lunch so I didn't need it that much either, but it was a decent and easy way to make money. That's why later on we started to wait until school ended and had lunch afterward.

So to conclude, this book, either directly or indirectly taught me about business and the value of money in one way or another.
