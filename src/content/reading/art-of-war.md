## Reflection

I had a decent-sized library back at home. Art of War was one of the books I was given by my parents which had been gathering dust for the longest time. I thought that you didn't really have to read the so-called classics, and in the case of Art of War, perhaps my opinion was correct.

What I gained from reading the books amounted to understanding human interaction at a macro level. While nearly every source of knowledge related to inter-person communication is on the personal level, perhaps due to being written by a strategist, Art of War has a different perspective. It explains how you can use preconcieved notions to your advantage; and how to interact with people who have more influence than you and do not heed your words.

Overall, it wasn't really something I thought would be useful, but perhaps it could be useful for people who are interested in the corporate or social ladder and would like some pointers from a man that knew the human psyche inside out. Even if the culture and time period are different, at our base, we are likely the same and similar, if not the same conventions apply.

There could be an argument that this is a very useful piece of knowledge to have when managing people, as that would move from the domain of individual contribution to management and strategy. From what I know from my Management 101 University Course, that would be beyond the scope of most middle managers, but would be a useful tool for fighting for the next promotion where these skills could become vital.

It could also be useful for gaining market dominance for those who run a business, but that isn't my area of expertise.
