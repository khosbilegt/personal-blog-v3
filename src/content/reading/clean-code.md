## Reflection

I read the book when I was still studying for my undergraduate degree but hadn't understood much of it. I believe it's one of those books that you don't really understand until you actually write production code, have to refactor it for some reason and then curse your past self for writing such shabby code.

During my first re-read, meaning my second time reading it, I found much to extract from it. While I cannot put into words what exactly I gained from it, it's not due to lack of it, but overall abundance. It explains what exactly makes for clean code, which I interpreted as "code that your future self won't hate, and even your least capable coworker would be able to edit with impunity".

Perhaps even during my first read, I learned some simple concepts and started incorporating them into my programming style, but those were things that the convenors and teaching assistants back at the National University of Mongolia had already started instilling into us from our first semester of coding.

What really felt useful was the overall best practice around scaffolding code and handling project structure cleanly, without making it cluttered, and hating on God Classes with improper naming. I'd started writing Java code for backend services in Quarkus (Spring Boot equivalent), so all the concepts could be easily applied in real-time.
